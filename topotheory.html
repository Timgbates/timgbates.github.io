So today we're gonna talk about Nil geometry. This will serve as an introduction to many of the things that we care about in differential geometry and topology. Consider the Heisenberg Group $$ \mathcal{H} = \left\{ \begin{pmatrix} 1 & x & z \\ 0 & 1 & y \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} | x, y, z \in \mathbb{R} \right\} $$ Topologically we can identify $\mathcal{H}$ as $\mathbb{R}^3$. But there's some more interesting structure than simply the topology (or smooth structre), namely the group structure. Notice that $$ \begin{pmatrix} 1 & a & c \\ 0 & 1 & b \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} 1 & x & z \\ 0 & 1 & y \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & a + x & c + z + ay \\ 0 & 1 & b + y \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} $$ which wrtten in a slightly different notation is $(a, b, c)*(x, y, z) = (a+x, b+y, c+z + ay)$. Thus it appears that this composition is almost a translation, except this additional term in the z-coordiante.

Let's take a step back and think. Given two points on a manifold, since I care about it up to topological or smooth deformations, I can stretch the two points out to appear seemingly far apart, or I could squeeze the space between them, making them appear close togeether. There is no well defined notion of distance between points in a manifold, so we need something else. Let's think back to Calc 3, how did we compute the length of a curve in $\mathbb{R}^3$? We used the fact that if you integrate an infitesimal velocity, you obtain an infitesimal measure of length of a curve, thus what you need to do in order to measure length is to integrate the velocities along the curve. Namely for a curve $\gamma: [a, b] \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^3$, you get $$ L(\gamma) = \int_a^b \sqrt{\left< \gamma'(t), \gamma'(t)\right>}dt $$ Of course, the reason why we can do this is because in the usual $\mathbb{R}^3$ from calculus, we have a natural way to measure the size of a vector, namely the square root of the usual inner product. So what we need on a manifold is a way to compute the sizes of velocity vectors of curves.

To address the first point, the collection of velocities of curves passing through a given point $p \in M$ is simply the tangent space denoted $T_pM$. The origin of this name can be seen by thinking back to computing the velocities of curves on a sphere in $\mathbb{R}^3$.
